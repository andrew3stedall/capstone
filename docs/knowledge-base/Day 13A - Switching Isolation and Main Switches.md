## Core concepts

- Functional switching changes normal operation; it does not by itself establish a safe work boundary.
- Protective operation, emergency switching and isolation have distinct purposes and must not be treated as interchangeable.
- Isolation is a bounded claim about separation from every relevant source and energisable path under an authorised safe-work process.
- A main-switch label is evidence of a defined switching role, not proof that every conductor and alternate source is separated.
- An isolation boundary identifies the equipment or board, sources, conductors, proposed means, stored energy and authorised verification point.
- OFF, open, stopped, de-energised and isolated describe different states or claims.
- Evidence is graded as observed, documented, verified, assumed or missing.
- Claims are graded as described, supported, verified or unresolved.

## Reasoning workflow

Use **S-E-P-A-R-A-T-E**:

1. **State** the intended purpose.
2. **Enumerate** every source and energy path.
3. **Place** the exact boundary.
4. **Assign** supported functions to devices.
5. **Retrieve** current authorised requirements.
6. **Assess** evidence quality.
7. **Test** the claim for completeness and changed conditions.
8. **Express** a bounded conclusion or stop.

The workflow deliberately ends at an evidence gate. It does not provide a practical switching, isolation, securing or verification sequence.

## Practical application

Use a fictional paper single-line diagram to:

- classify each device by apparent function;
- map normal, alternate, generated, stored, control and feedback sources;
- identify every energisable path crossing the intended boundary;
- draft precise boundary statements;
- grade drawings, labels and device data;
- reopen the analysis when a new source or altered arrangement is discovered;
- state what is supported, unresolved and prohibited pending authorised verification.

## Assessment relevance

A successful learner can distinguish device function from isolation evidence, map all stated and plausible source paths, write an exact boundary statement, grade evidence, reopen dependencies after a changed condition and stop without inventing a field procedure.

A 12-point educational rubric assesses:

- switching-versus-isolation classification;
- source and conductor completeness;
- boundary precision;
- evidence quality;
- changed-condition reasoning;
- safety-bounded conclusion.

Critical errors override the score: treating OFF as isolation, omitting a known source, claiming verified isolation from paper evidence, inventing a field sequence or proceeding with missing safety-critical evidence.
