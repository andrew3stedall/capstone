# 12-Week Day 06 - Evidence Quality Applicability and Completeness Workshop

## Purpose

This note connects Day 6 to the wider Capstone knowledge graph. The block develops source navigation into claim-centred evidence judgement: the learner must decide whether every material premise is supported by evidence that is trustworthy, applicable and complete enough for the exact conclusion.

## Core concepts

- **Evidence status:** each item is labelled as a stated fact, verified source statement, inference, assumption, contradiction, evidence gap or matter outside authority.
- **Evidence quality:** authority, currency, traceability, integrity and genuine independence of an item.
- **Applicability:** whether valid evidence governs the scenario’s jurisdiction, installation, supply, circuit, equipment, location, definitions, exceptions and authority conditions.
- **Completeness:** whether every material premise and upstream dependency required for a claim is supported.
- **Material premise:** an item that could change the conclusion if it changed or remained unknown.
- **Evidence dependency:** a downstream conclusion that must be reopened when an upstream item becomes invalid or uncertain.
- **Contradiction register:** a record of conflicting applicable evidence, scope checks, resolution or escalation ownership.
- **Bounded conclusion:** supported, conditionally supported, insufficient evidence or outside authority.
- **Evidence owner:** the person or role responsible for resolving a recorded gap.
- **Recheck trigger:** a material change that requires evidence and conclusions to be reviewed again.

## C-L-E-A-R workflow

1. **Claim** — separate the proposed answer into exact testable claims.
2. **Locate** — identify scenario facts, candidate controlling sources and plausible competing sources.
3. **Evaluate** — label evidence status and check authority, currency, traceability, integrity and independence.
4. **Apply** — map definitions, conditions, exceptions, dependencies and authority limits.
5. **Review** — test completeness, resolve or register contradictions and state a bounded outcome.

Passing one evidence test does not compensate for failing another. The overall conclusion cannot be stronger than its weakest unresolved material premise.

## Practical application

The learner:

- sorts fictional evidence by status and identifies what each item can and cannot support;
- maps conditions and upstream dependencies for one scenario;
- decomposes a broad protective-arrangement statement into exact claims and material premises;
- records contradictions rather than averaging them;
- changes two material conditions and identifies invalidated evidence, reopened dependencies, revised confidence, evidence owners and recheck triggers.

The changed-context task is essential. It prevents a correct first answer from being treated as transferable when the equipment, supply, jurisdiction, source edition or authority boundary changes.

## Assessment relevance

Observable evidence is criterion-level rather than an aggregate score. The learner must demonstrate:

- exact claim and premise definition;
- correct evidence-status labels;
- source-quality checks;
- an applicability and dependency map;
- a completeness audit;
- defensible conflict handling;
- a bounded conclusion;
- changed-context transfer.

A `stop-required` result, unsupported safety-critical claim or hidden blocking premise cannot be averaged away by stronger work elsewhere. This is an educational readiness rule, not an official RTO pass standard.
