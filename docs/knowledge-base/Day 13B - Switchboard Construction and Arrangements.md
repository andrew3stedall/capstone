# Day 13B - Switchboard Construction and Arrangements

Day 13B develops the source-and-boundary reasoning from [[Day 13A - Switching Isolation and Main Switches]] into an assembly-level review. It prevents the learner from judging one device, one empty position or one tidy photograph without considering the sources, functional zones, access boundaries, interactions and evidence that govern the whole switchboard.

## B-O-A-R-D-S workflow

1. **Bound** the assembly, connected sections, accessible sides, sources and operating states.
2. **Outline** incoming, switching, protection, distribution, neutral, protective-earthing, control and alternate-supply functions.
3. **Assess** access, barriers, segregation, conductor routes, heat, fault conditions, support, termination, identity and maintainability interactions.
4. **Retrieve** current authorised and manufacturer evidence.
5. **Decide** whether each claim is described, supported, verified or unresolved.
6. **Stress-test** one changed source, device, route, load or enclosure condition and reopen dependent conclusions.

## Core distinctions

### Assembly boundary

The assembly boundary defines the enclosure, connected sections, sources, internal functions and accessible sides included in the paper review. An undefined boundary makes later conclusions unstable.

### Functional zone

A functional zone is a learner-created reasoning area for grouping sources, switching, protection, distribution, neutral, protective-earthing, control and alternate-supply functions. It is not a universal physical layout.

### Access boundary

The access boundary separates what the supplied evidence allows the learner to examine from what would require opening, dismantling, testing or authorised site work.

### Coexistence and compatibility

Visible coexistence means components appear together. Compatibility requires applicable authorised evidence supporting their use together for the relevant assembly and conditions.

### Spare space and spare capacity

Spare space is visible physical room. Spare capacity is an evidence-backed conclusion considering electrical, thermal, fault, mechanical, termination, identification and manufacturer constraints.

## Evidence and claim grades

### Evidence grades

- **Observed:** directly visible in supplied material.
- **Documented:** stated in a current drawing, schedule, label or authorised record.
- **Manufacturer-verified:** supported by applicable assembly or compatibility information.
- **Assumed:** plausible but not evidenced.
- **Missing:** required for the conclusion but unavailable.

### Claim grades

- **Described:** states what the supplied material shows.
- **Supported:** combines applicable evidence into a bounded reasoning statement.
- **Verified:** requires complete authorised evidence and qualified confirmation appropriate to the claim.
- **Unresolved:** a material evidence gap prevents the claim.

## Practical application

For a fictional board extension:

1. state the assembly boundary;
2. list every source and operating state;
3. draw functional zones without inventing hidden construction;
4. grade each item of evidence;
5. review access, barriers, segregation, routing, heat, fault conditions, neutral and earth facilities, identification and maintainability;
6. request the missing authorised evidence;
7. write a bounded claim;
8. explain which conclusions reopen when an inverter, changed load or different board section is introduced.

The key conclusion pattern is:

> Physical space may be described. Suitability and spare capacity remain unresolved until the relevant evidence is complete.

## Assessment relevance

A strong response:

- defines a complete paper boundary and source inventory;
- maps functions and paths rather than listing devices;
- connects access, thermal, fault, compatibility and maintenance interactions;
- grades evidence and claims consistently;
- reopens every dependent conclusion after a change;
- states stop conditions without implying practical authority.

The module uses a six-category, 12-point educational rubric. A critical error overrides the numerical result. This is not an official assessment rule.

## Misconceptions to track

- A tidy board is compliant.
- An empty position proves spare capacity.
- Components that fit or appear together are compatible.
- A device rating proves the suitability of the whole assembly.
- A current schedule proves hidden construction.
- One source or one open switch defines every operating state.
- A paper review permits opening or testing the board.
- A changed source or load does not affect earlier conclusions.

## Safety boundary

This module authorises no switchboard access, cover removal, opening, probing, isolation, testing, measurement, alteration, repair, energisation, commissioning or verification. Unknown sources, conflicting documents, missing manufacturer evidence, suspected heat or damage, unclear neutral or protective-earthing arrangements and any task beyond the learner's authority require escalation.
