## Core relationships

- A **hazard** is a source of possible harm; **risk** considers exposure and consequence.
- Controls should act as directly as reasonably possible on the hazard, with elimination and engineering controls generally stronger than controls that depend mainly on behaviour or PPE.
- Equipment that is not operating is not automatically proven de-energised.
- Normal supply, alternative supply, stored energy, induced voltage, control circuits and mechanical energy may all affect the task boundary.
- A stop-and-escalate decision is required when authority, competence, equipment state, procedure or evidence is unclear.

## Practical application

Use the Day 2 safety-decision worksheet before an original inspection, testing or fault-finding scenario. Identify the task boundary, every relevant energy source, non-electrical hazards, governing sources, controls, verification evidence, residual risk and explicit stop conditions.

## Assessment relevance

A defensible response must distinguish a correct technical result from a safe process. Assessment evidence should show hazard recognition, control selection, verification, competence limits, reassessment when conditions change and escalation when uncertainty remains.
