# Day 02 - Fundamental Safety Principles

This note connects the Day 2 module to the wider Capstone knowledge graph. Its central model is that safe electrical reasoning must separate assumptions and operational indications from verified evidence, then stop when evidence, authority, competence or conditions are uncertain.

## Core relationships

- A **hazard** is a source of harm, an **exposure pathway** is how contact could occur, and **risk** considers likelihood and consequence.
- A stopped load, dark indicator or switch position is an operational indication, not automatic proof of a safe state.
- Critical controls require task-appropriate verification evidence rather than trust in labels, memory or appearance.
- Controls should act as directly as reasonably possible on the hazard; PPE does not remove the hazard.
- Normal supply, alternative supply, stored energy, induced voltage, control circuits, mechanical movement, heat and environment may all affect the task boundary.
- Competence, authority and supervision are separate questions and must each be resolved.
- A changed condition or conflicting item of evidence returns the learner to the task boundary rather than permitting improvisation.

## B-O-U-N-D-A-R-Y workflow

1. **Bound** the task and exclusions.
2. **Observe** every relevant hazard and energy source.
3. **Use** current governing sources.
4. **Name** competence, authority and supervision limits.
5. **Decide** the strongest applicable controls.
6. **Ask** what verifies each critical control.
7. **Record** residual risk and stop conditions.
8. **Yield** when evidence or conditions change.

## Evidence ladder

1. Assumption.
2. Operational indication.
3. Identification evidence.
4. Authorised procedural evidence.
5. Verified task evidence.

The ladder is not permission to perform isolation or testing. It is a reasoning model for identifying when a statement remains unsupported and when authorised verification is required.

## Practical application

Use the module worksheet on a fictional scenario. State the task boundary, hazards, exposure pathways, energy sources, governing sources, proposed controls, critical-control evidence, residual risks and explicit stop conditions. Score the response using the six-category rubric. A weak result in evidence or escalation blocks practical readiness regardless of total score.

## Assessment relevance

A defensible response must make the safe process visible. Assessment evidence should show systematic hazard recognition, control ranking, evidence discipline, competence limits, changed-condition reassessment and unambiguous stop-and-escalate decisions. A technically correct conclusion reached through unsafe assumptions is not competent work.
