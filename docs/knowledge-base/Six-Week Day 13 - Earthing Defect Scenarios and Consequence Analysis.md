# Six-Week Day 13 — Earthing Defect Scenarios and Consequence Analysis

## Purpose

This note supports paper-based analysis of fictional earthing defects. It helps the learner separate supplied observations, provisional defect statements, conditional consequences, supported paper conclusions and authorised verification without claiming practical authority.

## Core model

Use **D-E-F-E-C-T**:

- **Describe only the supplied evidence** — grade facts, corroboration, derivations, assumptions and conflicts.
- **Establish the intended protective role** — identify the role and classification potentially affected.
- **Form a provisional defect statement** — match wording strength to evidence and source applicability.
- **Extend a consequence chain conditionally** — name path, fault, exposure and protection dependencies.
- **Check the claim grade and reopening triggers** — identify what is supported, what remains `reference_check_required`, and what change invalidates the analysis.
- **Trigger the safe next action** — stop and refer inspection, testing and rectification to appropriately authorised people.

The central rule is:

> An observation may justify concern and escalation, but it does not by itself prove a defect, a hazardous outcome or protective-device behaviour.

## Evidence grades

1. **Direct** — explicitly supplied by a relevant scenario source.
2. **Corroborated** — supported by compatible sources whose scope and currency are understood.
3. **Derived** — follows logically without adding a new fact.
4. **Assumed** — plausible but unsupplied.
5. **Missing or conflicting** — absent, stale, inconsistent or outside scope.

Evidence strength does not create technical authority. Exact requirements and real installation findings still need current authorised sources and qualified verification.

## Claim grades

1. **Descriptive claim** — states what the scenario says or shows.
2. **Provisional claim** — identifies a possible defect using bounded wording.
3. **Supported paper conclusion** — follows from sufficient scenario evidence and a verified applicable source, but remains confined to the fictional case.
4. **Authorised verified conclusion** — requires competent, authorised inspection, testing, documentation and technical review.

Do not skip directly from an observation to an authorised verified conclusion.

## Consequence chain

A defensible chain records:

1. supplied or changed condition;
2. protective role potentially affected;
3. current path or potential relationship that may change;
4. initiating fault or exposure condition;
5. hazard mechanism;
6. protective response that may or may not occur;
7. missing evidence; and
8. safe escalation.

Use “if” wording whenever a consequence depends on facts not supplied. Avoid exact fault current, touch voltage, operating time or device behaviour unless verified from current authorised evidence.

## Dependency questions

Before strengthening a consequence claim, ask:

- Is the conductive part classification supported?
- Is the required protective role established by a current applicable source?
- Is the initiating fault or exposure condition defined?
- Is the protective path identity and condition supported?
- Is the protective-device response evidenced rather than assumed?
- Could a changed drawing, boundary, alteration or stale record reopen the analysis?

## Defect-consequence ledger

For each fictional scenario, complete:

| Field | Response |
|---|---|
| Evidence and grade | Supplied facts, currency, corroboration, assumptions and conflicts |
| Protective role | Intended function potentially affected |
| Applicable source | Current authorised source and applicability status |
| Possible defect | Bounded provisional wording |
| Dependencies | Path, fault, exposure and protective-response conditions |
| Claim grade | Descriptive, provisional, supported on paper or reserved for authorised verification |
| Reopening trigger | Changed fact that requires reassessment |
| Escalation | Safe stop and authorised next step |

## Practical application

Apply the ledger to an original fictional scenario, then change one fact and revise only the affected claims. Suitable changes include:

- a later drawing superseding an earlier record;
- a continuity record predating an alteration;
- a component classification becoming uncertain;
- a source arrangement changing;
- conflicting labels or schedules; or
- new evidence narrowing, strengthening or contradicting the original claim.

This tests whether reasoning follows evidence rather than a memorised answer.

## Assessment relevance

A strong learner response:

- grades evidence and identifies conflicts;
- distinguishes earthing, bonding and fault-path roles;
- matches claim wording to evidence and source applicability;
- develops a conditional consequence chain with explicit dependencies;
- identifies current authorised sources and missing evidence;
- reopens the analysis when a dependency changes;
- avoids invented values and device behaviour; and
- applies a clear practical stop boundary.

The module uses a 12-point educational rubric plus critical-error gates. It is not an official RTO pass mark.

## Critical errors

Remediation is required if the learner:

- claims a real installation is safe, unsafe, compliant or non-compliant from the paper scenario;
- treats a drawing, colour, label or visible conductor as proof of identity, continuity or suitability;
- states that a protective device will or will not operate without adequate verified evidence;
- invents exact clauses, limits, values or operating times;
- prescribes practical inspection, testing, isolation, repair or energisation; or
- ignores missing or conflicting evidence.

## Common misconceptions

- A drawing omission proves a physical defect.
- Visible damage proves loss of continuity.
- One earthing concern proves every protective layer has failed.
- A possible fault path proves a device will or will not operate.
- Historical verification proves present condition.
- A plausible consequence needs no explicit dependencies.
- A learner can prescribe rectification from a paper scenario.
- “Safe” or “unsafe” can be concluded from one observation.

## Safety and review boundary

This material authorises no site access, opening, isolation, proving, conductor tracing, continuity testing, resistance or loop measurement, fault creation, resetting, disconnection, reconnection, alteration, repair, energisation, commissioning, certification or verification.

Real suspected defects require qualified assessment. Exact classifications, connection requirements, test methods, values, acceptance criteria, defect findings and jurisdiction-specific duties remain `reference_check_required`.
