# Six-Week Day 02 — Hazard, Risk, Exposure and Critical Controls

## Purpose

This note connects the six-week program's source hierarchy to evidence-led safety reasoning. The learner separates observations from assumptions, maps how a hazard could reach a person or asset, tests whether critical controls are actually effective and stops when authority or control evidence is unresolved.

## Core model

Use **S-C-O-P-E**:

- **State the scenario** — identify the task, people, equipment, environment, energy sources and observed facts.
- **Choose the controlling source family** — locate the current legislation, regulator guidance, authorised standard, network requirement, manufacturer information, RTO direction or approved workplace procedure governing the decision.
- **Outline the exposure chain** — connect hazard, initiating event or condition, exposure pathway and plausible consequence.
- **Prove the controls** — test critical controls for presence, applicability, integrity, independence and authorised verification.
- **Escalate uncertainty** — stop when authority, equipment state, isolation status, control integrity or source applicability is unresolved.

The reusable chain is:

**hazard → initiating event or condition → exposure pathway → consequence → controls → verification → residual uncertainty**

## Core distinctions

### Hazard and risk

A hazard is a source or situation with potential to cause harm. Risk combines the possibility of an event with the severity of its consequences in the actual context.

### Initiating event and exposure pathway

The initiating event or condition allows the hazard to enter the pathway. The exposure pathway is the route by which the hazard could reach a person, equipment or property.

### Critical control

A critical control is one whose absence or failure could materially permit a severe outcome. Its presence is not enough; its effectiveness requires evidence.

### Control-state questions

For each critical control ask:

1. Is it present?
2. Does it apply to the actual hazard and pathway?
3. Is it intact and not bypassed or degraded?
4. Is it independent of another unverified control or assumption?
5. Is its effectiveness verified by the authorised process?
6. Is any material question unresolved?

A visible control can still be inapplicable, damaged, dependent or unverified.

## Evidence grades

- **Observed:** directly visible in supplied material.
- **Documented:** stated in a current authorised record or procedure.
- **Verified:** supported by the authorised process appropriate to the claim.
- **Assumed:** plausible but not evidenced.
- **Missing:** required for the decision but unavailable.

## Practical application

For each paper-based scenario, record:

1. observed facts;
2. hazard source;
3. initiating event or condition;
4. exposure pathway;
5. plausible consequence;
6. controls and intended functions;
7. critical controls and their control states;
8. authority and supervision boundary;
9. residual uncertainty;
10. decision to continue in simulation, obtain evidence, or stop and escalate.

A changed-scenario re-attempt should alter the environment, exposed person, supply arrangement or control condition so the learner must rebuild the analysis rather than copy corrections.

## Assessment relevance

A strong response:

- keeps observations separate from assumptions;
- connects the complete exposure chain;
- explains the function of every named control;
- tests critical controls for applicability, integrity, independence and verification;
- identifies the applicable source family and authority boundary;
- reaches a bounded decision from the evidence;
- changes the conclusion when a material scenario condition changes.

The module uses a six-category, 12-point educational rubric with critical-error gates. It is not an official RTO marking scheme.

## Misconceptions to track

- Hazard and risk mean the same thing.
- A control is effective because it is visible.
- Two controls are independent because they have different names.
- PPE automatically proves the risk is adequately controlled.
- An off position proves every hazardous source is controlled.
- Missing safety-critical evidence can be filled from experience.
- A paper exercise permits practical access or testing.
