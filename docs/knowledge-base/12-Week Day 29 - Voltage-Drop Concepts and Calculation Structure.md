# 12-Week Day 29 - Voltage-Drop Concepts and Calculation Structure

## Purpose

Builds an evidence-controlled voltage-drop calculation structure while keeping arithmetic, interpretation, suitability and compliance claims separate.

## Core concepts

- **Calculation boundary:** the defined start and end points included in one result.
- **Operating case:** the equipment state, loading combination and current condition represented by the calculation.
- **Physical route length:** the stated or measured one-way route distance.
- **Path convention:** how the selected method represents the electrical path; it must not be inferred from route length alone.
- **Conductor data:** sourced resistance, reactance, impedance or tabulated information whose applicability remains attached.
- **Upstream contribution:** voltage drop before the present calculation boundary.
- **Provenance:** the source, edition, location and applicability record for an input or method.
- **First unsupported transition:** the earliest step not supported by adequate evidence; dependent later claims remain unsupported.
- **Recheck trigger:** a change that reopens completed reasoning or calculation steps.
- **Dimensional check:** confirmation that units combine to produce the intended result unit.

## Evidence states

Use [[Evidence Quality]] to classify each material input or claim as:

- stated fact;
- derived fact;
- supported inference;
- assumption;
- contradiction; or
- evidence gap.

An assumption does not become a fact because it appears in a completed calculation table.

## V-O-L-T-S workflow

1. **Verify** the boundary and operating case.
2. **Obtain** an authorised method and applicable conductor data.
3. **Lock** the path convention separately from physical route length.
4. **Transform** visibly through substitution, conversion, arithmetic and dimensional checking.
5. **State** a bounded result with unresolved contributions, criteria and contradictions named.

The workflow links [[Voltage Drop]], [[Cable Selection]], [[Circuit Design]], [[Dimensional Analysis]] and [[Evidence Quality]].

## Claim ladder

A defensible result progresses through these distinct claims:

1. supported boundary;
2. supported operating case;
3. supported conductor and route identity;
4. applicable method and data;
5. controlled path convention and units;
6. reproducible calculation;
7. bounded result; and
8. authorised interpretation.

The first unsupported transition stops dependent downstream claims. Strong arithmetic cannot repair an unsupported conductor identity, route record or method.

## Practical applications

Learners:

- build an input and evidence register;
- audit provenance, units and applicability;
- compare conflicting route and operating-current records;
- complete a fictional visible calculation;
- use an independent reasonableness check rather than repeating the same calculator entry;
- identify the first unsupported transition;
- propagate two changed material conditions through dependent steps; and
- write a bounded result statement.

## Assessment relevance

Observable evidence includes:

- accurate boundary and operating-case definition;
- correct evidence classification;
- source and applicability control;
- separation of physical route length from path convention;
- transparent substitution, conversion and dimensional checking;
- contradiction handling without convenient record selection;
- reopening of affected steps after changed inputs;
- confidence calibration; and
- claim restraint.

Criterion-level educational states are **secure**, **developing**, **unsupported** and `stop-required`. They are not official grades, competency decisions or legal classifications. Blocking safety, authority, contradiction and evidence failures cannot be offset by stronger arithmetic elsewhere.
